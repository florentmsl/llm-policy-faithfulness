**Baseline (original Robot Pong)**  
The policy acts as a reactive vertical‑tracking controller with occasional serving actions.  
- When the ball is noticeably **above** the player paddle (`state['LT(Player1, Ball1).y']` small/negative), the nested tests eventually return **LEFT** (move up).  
- When the ball is noticeably **below** the paddle, the tests return **RIGHT** (move down).  
- If the ball is already close vertically to the paddle (the distance checks on `D(Player1,Ball1).y` etc. fall inside the small‑range branches), the policy returns **NOOP** (wait).  
- When the ball is far away or just after a point (the `ED(... )` and `C(... )` checks that serve as “far‑from‑ball” guards fire), the policy returns **FIRE** (serve) or a combined move‑and‑fire (`LEFTFIRE`/`RIGHTFIRE`) to launch the ball while slightly biasing the paddle upward or downward.  
Overall, the agent continuously tries to keep its paddle aligned with the ball’s y‑coordinate, serving only when the ball is out of reach, which yields a competent rally‑and‑return behavior against a moving opponent.

**Simplified (opponent paddle lazy when ball is on player’s side)**  
The same tracking logic remains active, but because the opponent paddle stops moving as soon as the ball crosses to the player’s half, the agent no longer needs to aim precisely to get the ball past the opponent.  
- The vertical tracking (LEFT/RIGHT/NOOP) still moves the paddle to meet the ball, ensuring a hit almost every time.  
- Since the opponent will not react, the agent’s occasional **FIRE** or **LEFTFIRE/RIGHTFIRE** actions now serve mainly to reset the ball after a point; they are less critical for scoring.  
- Consequently, the agent returns the ball far more often and misses far fewer times, leading to a higher rally length and a greater chance to reach the 21‑point win condition. In short, the policy’s behavior is unchanged, but the simplified opponent dynamics make its actions far more effective.
